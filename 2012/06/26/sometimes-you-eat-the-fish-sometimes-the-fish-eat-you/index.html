sometimes you eat the fish. sometimes the fish eat you.

Posted on June 26, 2012 by ali

After taking in as much marine life as our novice lungs could take, we migrated inward from the coast to the jungle, the river Kinabatangan. Borneo is home to one of the oldest rainforests in the world which in turn houses many endangered, beautiful, and (in the spirit of full disclosure) somewhat homely creatures.

We traveled to Kota Kinabatangan and after various forms of transportation we finally arrived at our lodge, nestled under the jungle’s towering canopy. We started our day there with a “river cruise” where our guide navigated a small boat up and down the big brown river. Seconds after we began, as is the way in the rainforest, a torrential downpour began. Despite the sheets of water tumbling into our boat, within minutes we happened upon a troop of proboscis monkeys . The proboscis monkeys, whose name in Malay means “Dutchman” — a less than flattering reference to the noses and bellies of their colonizers, lounged in big trees right on the river bank. When we caught their gaze, they were sure to turn their backs to us as if trying to avoid the snickers that their bulbous noses and overgrown bellies provoke.

As we continued down river, we saw a magnificent pygmy elephant eating leaves on the water’s edge. I was aggravated with our guide, Helson, who hardly slowed the boat as we passed by. But, he knew what he was doing. Where there’s one elephant, there are more. Just around the bend, 6 adult, 2 adolescent, and 2 newborn elephants bathed in mud on the river banks. We pulled up, and sat in awed silence mere feet from the creatures (whose name “pygmy” doesn’t do justice to their enormity). So impressed with the experience, I was sure that the next 3 days would be a let-down. What could possibly live up to seeing a pack of wild elephants (and wild baby elephants at that!) so close-up?

Yet, the jungle was literally shaking with life. everywhere you looked leaves and branches were swaying, bending, and sometimes breaking under the weight of monkeys fighting, grooming, playing, and satisfying other less polite urges. Birds flew overhead, and the jungle resonated with the sounds of frogs and cicadas and the smell of wild boar.

The next two days were filled with treks in the jungle and cruises down the river filled with wildlife. We saw wild boars, pig-tailed macaques , long-tailed macaques , rhino hornbills , and eagles. Once the sun went down, the jungle transformed — a veritable changing of the guard. Birds, monkeys, and elephants settled in for the night, and snakes, crocodiles, bats, and other carnivores claimed the jungle as their own. During night hikes adam’s extensive childhood training in snake hunting payed off — he spotted a slug eating snake which impressed even our guide. We also saw birds sleeping on their nests, impressively large insects, and even a Malay civet (which adam was convinced was just a house cat gone feral in the jungle).

On our last full day, after a long hike, we put our feet in a lake, only to be swarmed by thousands upon thousands of small fish, devouring the bacteria and dead skin from hands and feet. We were told that people PAY for this kind of treatment in European spas: “it’s exfoliating”…”really brings you back to nature”. Ick.

During a sunset trip down the river the night before we left, we all silently hoped to see the elusive, endangered orangutan (which in Malay means “man of the forest”). And — remarkably– we did. Two black masses silhouetted in the heights of a fig tree slowly began to move. And move closer. Soon, we could make out a mom and her child, leisurely spanning out their seemingly indeterminable limbs to grab figs and pop them one by one into their mouths. The sun began to set, and we watched as the orangutans climbed out of the fig tree and into the tree directly in front of us in order to construct a nest where they’d pass the night. Branch by branch, the nest was made. And soon they tucked themselves in for the night. And so, contented and covered in scabs from leeches and patches of mud, we did the same.
