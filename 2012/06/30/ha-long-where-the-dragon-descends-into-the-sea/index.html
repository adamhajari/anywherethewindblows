Ha Long: where the dragon descends into the sea

Posted on June 30, 2012 by adam

According to Vietnamese legend, a giant dragon once lived in the mountains on the northern coast of Vietnam. As it flew down the mountain to dive into the sea, its tail bounced along the tops of the hills carving out steep canyons leading into the ocean. As the pockets left by the dragon’s tail filled with shimmering emerald green water from the Gulf of Tonkin, it created a bay so magical that it gives even the most ardent skeptic pause to contemplate his disbelief in myths upon seeing its beauty. This is Halong Bay (literally, where the dragon descends into the sea).

We did little planning before leaving the States, but everything we saw and read convinced us to put Halong Bay on our list of places we had to see in Southeast Asia. However, after a negative experience on Khao San road in Bangkok (hoards of irreverent westerners whose overwhelming presence destroyed nearly all traces of authentic Thai culture) we’d decided we needed to make an effort to stray from the beaten paths suggested by most travel guide books. We’d heard that boat tours out of Halong City, while beautiful, are tarnished by both trash in the water and carts of Asian kitsch being sold to the bus loads of western tourists.

We decided instead to launch our exploration of Halong Bay and its equally beautiful neighbor, Lan Ha Bay, from the less easily accessible town of Cat Ba, located on an island in the southern part of Halong Bay. Once in Cat Ba we booked an overnight boat tour of the Bay leaving the following morning.

At 8 am we took a short bus ride to the port of departure and boarded our boat, a motorized junk with sleeping quarters, a bathroom, and a kitchen on the bottom level and an area for lounging, dining, and playing cards on the top level. Our crew consisted of a driver, a cook, and a “tour guide” (who spent 75% of the trip sleeping), and we were joined by three additional passengers, a group of travelers in their 20s from New Zealand.

As we left the crowded fishing port, we began to pass small islands of limestone rock, partially covered by green vegetation, jutting more than 100 m straight out of the water. The bay is densely populated with these limeston monuments, some like tall buildings, others like fortress walls.

After a few hours of cruising through the bay and snapping picture after picture that we knew couldn’t do it justice, we stopped at one of the larger islands, climbed a few dozen stairs half way to the top and found a cave illuminated by generator powered lights. 50 m in, the cave opened again to the outside revealing a gigantic pool of still green water completely surrounded by a ring of towering limestone.

The rest of the two days wast spent eating delicious freshly caught sea food, napping and reading on the top deck, escaping the heat with plunges into the ocean, and exploring more caves and secluded pools and beaches by kayak. In the evening after our first day on the water, the air had cooled and the sky was clear. We pulled down the sheet which had provided shade in the heat of the day and dragged a couple of mattresses from the sleeping quarters to the top deck, laid on our backs and fell asleep looking for shooting stars.
