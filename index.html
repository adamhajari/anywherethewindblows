22 flights, 10 countries, and 11 weeks later

Posted on August 19, 2012 by ali
1

After the cool mountain air and restful pace of Ladakh, we headed southeast to our penultimate destination: the holy city of Varanasi. We arrived before 5:30 am via a flight from Leh and overnight train from Delhi. The city still slept, wrapped in its own filth which wouldn’t be swept aside or washed away until its denizens awoke hours later. Stray dogs, holy cows, and troops of monkeys pilfered through trash which filled the street and poured over into shop openings and houses’ stoops. Narrow roads (barely large enough for you and the truculent bovine who seems pleased to scoot closer to you, bang you into a wall, and then swat you with its fecal-ful tail) wind around brightly colored buildings, so serpentine that after an hour one is left dismayed as to how she’s returned to where she began.

Varanasi is a city of profound religious import, said to have been founded by the Hindu God, Lord Shiva 3,000 years ago (placing it as a contender for the oldest continuously habitated city in the world). Hindus believe it is a place where Moksha (or, the “final release” from the chain of reincarnation) may be achieved. They also believe that the river Ganges represents (and for some sects, IS) the goddess Shakti, and that bathing in her waters erases all sins and purifies the soul. Varanasi is also a pilgrimage site for Buddhists as it is believed that it was here that Siddhartha Gautama gave his first sermon to his disciples, making it the birthplace of Buddhism.

This is a city where pilgrims sit on the ubiquitous mats of priests (whose holy credibility might be called in to question by the sheer quantity of them) to have their palms read and their fortunes deciphered. A city where under every arm there is a water bottle filled with the murky brown water of the Ganges, a souvenir to bring home. A city where monkeys roam freely through the temples, filching bags of food and screeching at devotees.

Here, life centers around the approximately one hundred ghats (or, broad flights of stairs that connect the city to the river’s waters) which lead worshippers down to the holy Ganges river. There are ghats for every purpose: holy bathing, performing the puja, and even two specifically for cremations. We spent the day roaming these ghats, ambivalent about the crush of sound, color, and smells. Then, drenched in sweat and looking for some creature comforts, we hired a bicycle rickshaw driver to take us on a 3 hour tour of the city (the going rate: 40 rupees/ $0.80 an hour).

After dinner, we wandered through the maze north, hoping to find the funeral ghats. The small cobbled stone alleyways were pitch black with the only light escaping from the small doors of houses, where families huddled together around shared dinner plates. We finally smelled smoke, and soon enough we were standing on the bank of the river, surrounded by walls of large logs used to fuel the cremation fires. This ghat – Manikarnika – draws devout hindus from across India (and beyond) to burn their loved ones above the Ganges river, hoping that such an auspicious passing will allow their souls to escape the cycle of reincarnation and ascend into a heavenly realm. There were nearly a dozen fires roaring. Male family members dressed in all white and prayed around the fires, watching the smoke, perhaps wondering if somewhere in the grey cloud was the soul of the person they loved.  We looked on in awe for hours, feeling like interlopers but too moved, too curious to leave.

According to a man we met, the cremation fires roar all day and all night, totaling about 200 cremations per day. When the body is burned (which takes approximately 3 hours), the family gathers up the bones and ash and throws them into the Ganges. However, because wood is expensive, some families cannot afford to fully burn their dead, leaving large pieces of bodies to float in the river. Additionally, women who are pregnant, children, those with leprosy, and those who died of snake bites are never burned (as it is thought that they have not yet completed a full life and thus must come back into the world and try again) and instead are tied to large rocks and thrown whole into the river. As a result (rumor has it), the local authorities began breeding and training flesh-eating turtles to consume the decomposing bodies which had begun to pose a threat to public health.

Then on to our final destination: the Taj Mahal. While neither of us was too excited to battle the heat, crowds, and hustlers of Agra (a notoriously terrible city to visit), no visit to India would be complete without it. Although Indians pay 20 rupees to gain entrance into the monument, foreign visitors pay 750 rupees ($15 USD, about the price of a hotel room with air conditioning for the night). The Taj Mahal was built in the early 1600’s by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan as a monument to his love for his 2nd (of 3) wife (Mumtaz Mahal) who died delivering her 14th child. The mausoleum was constructed with beautiful white marble, with ruby, jade, turquoise, lapis lazuli, sapphire, and onyx inlaid into the marble as flowers and Koranic text. Up close, the tomb comes alive with color.

Thus, having closed our time in India with the most quintessentially Indian tourist experience, we began our 5 day journey home. And so ends our 22 flight, 10 country, 11 week adventure.  Thanks for reading!

Ladakh: It’s All Downhill From Here

Posted on August 7, 2012 by adam
Reply

In the early hours of July 30th Ali and I were sound asleep in a hotel room in Delhi when we were awoken by a loud pop.  We looked up to see that the air conditioning unit mounted on the wall above our bed was off. Having suffered through many similar events since arriving in India, we sighed disappointingly and dozed off again. Cities across India lose power daily – typically for several hours at a time. Though, in most cases, “lose” power is inaccurate. It seems more often it’s the case that the power is turned off strategically in order to avoid grid overloads. In Rajasthan while waiting to check email at an internet cafe during a power outage, we asked someone when the electricity might come back on and he replied, “whenever the powermen decide to turn it back on”. On the morning of July 30th, however, powermen across India seemed uncoordinated as Ali and I unknowingly became participants in a series of power outages which affected half of India’s population and more than 9% of the world’s population (yeah, there’s a lot of people in India). Half an hour later we were in a taxi on our way to the Delhi airport. The power was still out across the country, but our driver soared through intersections once regulated by traffic lights using only his horn to let other vehicles know that, in his opinion, he had the right away. Luckily traffic was light at 3:30 in the morning and we arrived at the airport with out a problem.

We’d been in this same airport just a week earlier during a layover en route to Jodhpur. Surprisingly, despite lack of grid power, the airport was operating much the same as the previous week. Ticket counters were still printing tickets, televisions screens were still displaying departures and arrivals (most on time), and McDonald’s was still serving non-beef, non-pork burgers and breakfast sandwiches. As it turns out, the Indira Gandhi Airport boasts one of the world’s largest diesel powered generators, and within seconds of the outage 90% of the airport facilities were back up and functioning normally. Despite the unprecedented outage, the cogs that keep Delhi in motion continued to turn. The city was so unphased by the historical event that it wasn’t until a week later when checking emails for the first time since the outage that, by family inquiries about whether we were alright, we knew that anything out of the ordinary had happened at all.

Our plane was on the ground in Leh, a peaceful city tucked deep in the Himalayan Mountains, before the rush hour chaos resulting from lack of power in Delhi began. Leh is the capital of Ladakh, a region in India’s northern most state, Jamu & Kashmir and is unlike the rest of India in almost every way. The majority of India is either Hindu or Muslim, while Ladakh, which takes in many Tibetan refugees (and whose culture, food, dress, and language seems indistinguishable from Tibet’s), is predominantly Buddhist. The streets of most Indian cities are a chaotic mess of busses, cars, motorcycles, and people all pushing to the front of an endless sea of bodies and horns. Leh’s streets, while busy with shops and restaurants, are peaceful and horn-free with vehicles respecting single file lanes, traffic signs, and pedestrian spaces. And while Ladakh, like Rajasthan, is technically a desert, the landscape is composed of beautiful snow capped mountains below crystal blue skies. And the towns, through carefully constructed irrigation systems, are lush green oasises in the valleys of barren mountains. Finally, while the rest of India is either sweltering or scorching hot in the summer, Leh, nestled high in the Himalayan Mountains, has light-jacket weather in the mornings and evenings and comfortably warm shorts and t-shirt weather in the afternoons.

Ladakh’s altitude has disadvantages as well. Sea-level dwellers commonly experience headaches, nausea, dizziness, and feel like complete wimps when walking up small hills. We spent the morning and early afternoon of our first day rearranging our train and flight schedule to accommodate a 5 day trek from Lamayuru to Chilling ending with a whitewater rafting trip down the Zanskar River. By pushing back our return flight to Delhi we were able to start the trek 2 days later, giving us much needed time to adjust to the altitude and recover from the debilitating symptoms of altitude sickness.

Early on August 1st my headache and lightheadedness had subsided, and my blue lips regained their pinkish hue (though Ali and I were still getting winded walking up even modest hills). A taxi drove Ali, me, our friend Natalie, and our trekking guide the four hours along the motion sickness inducing, half-finished road from Leh to Lamayuru to start our trek. The two days of rest before starting our trek were essential, but several more would have been better. As a result of a decrease in air density, the available amount of oxygen to sustain normal mental and physiological activity decreases around 3000 m. High altitude is defined as 1500 m to 3500 m and very high altitude as 3500 m to 5500 m. Lamayuru sits at 3400 m (a mile higher than Denver Colorado), and on several occasions the trek took us over mountain passes of nearly 5000 m (over 3 miles above sea level).

The first two days were relatively easy (and I must emphasize that this is relative to days 3 and 4). Lamayuru, where we began our trek, is a small town with a beautiful monastery high in the hills. Around the monastery, old men and women sit spinning handheld prayer wheels, while Tibetan prayer flags flap in the wind, carrying away prayers into the air. Each night of the trek we slept in homes in villages along the way with the amount of walking each day determined by the distances between adjacent villages. Not a problem for days 1 and 2. Lamayuru is only a 4 hour hike to Wanla and Wanla only a 6 hour hike to Hinju. But Sumdho Chinmu, the next village beyond Hinju is a 12 hour hike separated by a 4950 m mountain pass. Halfway up the mountain our pace abruptly slowed. We’d take a hundred small steps before becoming completely out of breath (read: unable to breathe), necessitating frequent stops to sit on the path, catch our breath, and wait for the lightheadedness lessen. As we climbed higher on the steep and narrow mountain-side path, a hundred steps between stops shrank to 50, then 25. As we increased out altitude our step sizes became smaller and the number of steps between stops decreased as the amount of oxygen we were able to suck into our lungs gradually decreased with each step forward. For both of us, the experience was frustrating, and hands-down one of the most challenging physical feats of our lives. Once at the top we collapsed and closed our eyes for half an hour before continuing on to Sumdho Chinmu (luckily most of the 5+ hour descent was downhill). We arrived after 6 in the evening and went straight to bed.

The next day was similar in length and difficulty. Though we climbed two passes on the fourth day, the higher of the two was a mere 4700 m. Whether it was the 10 hours of sleep, an extra day of aclimitisation, or that our tallest ascent was 250 m less high than the previous day’s, the 4th day seemed significantly easier than the previous. And though difficult, days 3 and 4 were also beautiful. The views from atop the passes were remarkable. Surrounded by white topped mountains and hills of red and green rocks, we enjoyed a near infinite view of the foliaged valleys far below.

On day 4 we made a 1500 m (approximately 1 mile) descent from the day’s second pass to Chilling, a village on the Zanskar River. The descent was epic with some mountains resembling a lunar landscape, and others the sinister jagged rocked Mordor. The next morning we put aside our hiking boots and walking sticks in exchange for wet suits and paddles and started down the Zanskar. The rapids seemed mild, but the water, which originates from the snow capped mountains that surround the river, was as ice cold as the glacier it started from. Natalie and I sat in the front and were perpetually soaked with the bone chilling glacial water. Half way down the river we pulled ashore for a break and Natalie, who had had enough cold water for the day, gave up her spot in the front to an Austrian man convinced that living in the alps made him a better candidate for the position. Within 5 minutes of casting off again a rapid tilted our raft throwing our Austrian friend (and most of the rest of the boat) into the icy grey water. We were able to pull everyone back into the boat within a minute, but the fellow from Austria spent the rest of the trip down the river shivering and making good natured comments through clenched teeth about how much colder it was in the front of the raft

Cold, wet, and utterly exhausted, we returned to Leh. We decided to spend some time looking for a hotel with hot water (something that our previous hotel hadn’t). We walked from hotel to hotel, confused by the near ubiquitous “no vacancy”. Indeed, something about Leh had changed while we were high in the mountain passes. The streets were jammed packed with cars, westerners, and a proliferation of scarlet and saffron clad monks. It took us only a few minutes to realize that Leh had doubled in size due to a five day visit from the Dalai Lama.

The next day we got a taxi early in the morning to drive us the 9 km to the monastery where the Dalai Lama would be speaking. Ladakh’s tranquility was replaced with carnivalesque excitement and the frenetic energy of thousands of people all trying to snag a good seat. The Dalai Lama made jokes, gave suggestions on improved monastic pedagogy, and even kicked out a monk in the front row who he chided for “not paying attention”. More impressive than his holiness’s words, however, were the thousands of men, women, and children flooding in from surrounding areas turning the once quiet town of Leh into a city so loud and chaotic that it began to resemble the rest of India.

Western Rajasthan: some like it hot

Posted on July 29, 2012 by adam
2

Having had our fill of green jungles and palm tree lined beaches, we left south India for the hotter, dryer climate of Rajasthan’s deserts. We flew into Rajasthan’s western airport in Jodhpur, arriving in the early afternoon (the hottest time of day). Dismayed to find that the guesthouse that we’d booked the previous week only had one air conditioned room, which was already occupied (that’ll teach us to plan ahead), we locked up our bags and made our way to Jodhpur’s main attraction, Mehrangarh, a giant fort built from the rock on which it stands towering above the rest of the city. We made the steep climb to the top of the fort accompanied by the serenations of Jodhpur’s many street musicians bowing hand-made stringed instruments, pounding on drums, and blowing into high pitched flutes, and were rewarded with magnificent views of “the blue city” at the top.

The city’s well deserved nickname describes the color of the concrete building, a majority painted top to bottom with cheap blue paint that rubs off on your arms and legs as you pass so that newcomers, looking like blue man group rejects, are easily spotted (no pun intended). Ali quickly became enamored with Jodhpurs colorful buildings, photogenic people, and hectic narrow streets. Wandering around the main bazaar, we stumbled on a spice shop offering cooking classes. We returned the next day for a six hour cooking marathon, learning how to make many of our favorite Indian dishes. We were shown the secrets of delicious chai masala (brewed in whole milk), and cheese naan (made with yogurt rather than water), as well as given recipes and shown how to prepare palak paneer and tikka masala. And of course we ate everything we made. We left at midnight (two hours later than we’d planned) with bellies full and our pants tighter than when we’d arrived.

The next day we arrived in Jaisalmer, a touristy town near India’s western border with Pakistan. From the minute we left the train station we were hounded with mobs of rickshaw drivers and shop owners with rupee signs in their eyes at the sight of tourists. “Sir, come with me. I will take you to a nice hotel. Only 10 rupees”. “No, come with me. Rooms with AC. Veeearry cheap!”. “Please ma’am. Come into my shop. No need to buy. Just have a look”. Polite no thank you’s gradually becoming stern No’s then decaying into a furrow browed, head shaking, tunnel vision quest for a clean quiet room with air conditioning.

We took shelter at Hotel Paradise, showering and basking in the AC before heading back out to make arrangements for a two and half day camel safari into the heart of the desert. The next morning we pushed the start of our dessert trek back a few hours in order to enjoy the morning of Ali’s birthday lackadaisically with a birthday cake breakfast (Jaisalmer is not the best place to get a birthday cake btw), an ayurvedic message, and a shaded rooftop lunch.

By the afternoon we’d taken a jeep 30 minutes southwest of Jaisalmer to meet our camels and camelman, Mr Khan. A double seater saddle was strapped to each of our camels. We sat in the front seat atop our bedding while our (and our camel’s) supply of food and water for the next two and a half days sat behind us.

On a whole the safari was a little miserable. Riding a camel was far less comfortable than we had imagined, jerking us violently forward and then backwards with every step. On days 1 and 3 we trodded past hundreds of giant windmills which, while beautiful in their own right, seemed amiss among the desert landscape. Additionally, the hours between 10 am and 5 pm were HOT (capital H, O, and T), and midnight to 4 am, what would have otherwise been prime sleeping hours, were actually prime power generating hours with heavy winds blowing buckets of sand over us so that we became human shaped sand castles by morning.

At sundown, however, the air cooled quickly and the hours from sunset to a couple of hours after we laid down under the stars to go to sleep were warm, but pleasant. Mr. Khan baked chapattis, boiled rice, and cooked potato curry and we ate our dinner and drank chai while we watched the round yellow sun fade away unceremoniously behind the horizon’s thick cloud of dust.

all the south india you can fit into 5 days. and more.

Posted on July 21, 2012 by ali
Reply

From Sultan Bathery we headed to Ooty, a hill station in the tea growing mountains of Tamil Nadu. Ooty is a popular vacation destination for Indian tourists – particularly honeymooners. High in the Nilgiris, Ooty is a bustling and colorful city – an oasis in the undulating hills of tea and pine trees. We arrived via a 5 hour standing-room-only bus ride which violently curved around sharp bend after sharp bend. Only because Ooty was so beautiful did I forgive the nauseating hours during which my face was sandwiched between the odorous armpits of strangers.

Although we came to Ooty expecting to do some trekking, we had trouble finding anything to do there but packaged tours. So, we spent our two days enjoying the cold, drinking absurd quantities of masala tea, and roaming the byzantine bazaar. We met some wonderful people in and around the bazaar, developed the photos we took, and went back the next day to distribute them to our new friends. It was a nice way to thank all those people whose faces often thanklessly dot our blog. The one “day hike” we attempted was to the region’s highest peak – about 10km from our hotel. Although we shared the path with menacing (and loudly honking) busses and semi-trucks, the scenery was beautiful. However, the top of the peak wasn’t the peaceful summit we had anticipated. Rather, it was teeming with local tourists and groups of shrieking school girls. So, although it took us hours to climb to the peak, we didn’t linger but ten minutes at the top.

We shed our many warm layers and headed south to Kanyakumari – the southernmost tip of India. Along the shore dozens of stalls hawk spiced mango and ice cream, tourists snap photos of the water – a confluence of three seas, and pilgrims file in to the many Hindu temples. Just off the shore, boats shuttle visitors to two impressive islets, a monument for a monk and another for a poet. We woke up early to watch the sun rise to the sounds of blaring traditional music from two neighboring temples – a shouting contest of sorts.

Off we were to our next destination: Alleppey. Alleppey (or Alappuzha) is the top destination for “backwater” houseboat cruises. Although our guidebook states that while houseboat cruises “could be one of your most expensive experiences in India, it is usually worth every rupee” — and it did cost many rupees — we both found it relaxing but underwhelming put up against the awe inspiring beauty of Vietnam’s Ha Long Bay. At 11am we boarded our boat, which had a beautiful front room with two day beds, 2 lounging chairs, and a table for dining. Behind was a room with a bathroom and shower. The boat was like a hotel on water. There were three staff (a cook, a captain, and a third man whose duties may have only consisted of chain smoking hand rolled cigarettes on the ship’s helm) and us. We spent the day drifting through small channels, reading D.H. Lawrence (me) and Salman Rushdie (adam), and watching people fish, bathe, or wash their clothes on the river’s edge. At sunset we anchored in a village that smelled of ghee sizzling in hot pans and spent some time strolling, snapping photos, and watching king fishers and giant bats fly overhead.

When we returned to dry land 22 hours after setting sail, we hopped on a bus headed north to Kochi. Slumped over from the oppressive heat and the weight of our ever-growing bags (I do have a soft spot for saris…), we splurged and checked-in to a “business class” hotel. We plan to spend the next 36 hours taking hot showers, ordering room service, using wifi, and watching HBO. A needed vacation from our vacation….

Adam and Ali are “It’s Complicated” with India.

Posted on July 14, 2012 by ali
1

We arrived into Mumbai with uncertain plans. Although we had intended to take a train south to the southern state of Kerala directly upon arrival, we discovered that India does not make it easy for foreigners to travel by rail (or do a number of other quotidian tasks which surprisingly require proof of residency). After days of confusion regarding how to actually book a ticket, we soon realized that we had made another mistake — Indian trains are perpetually full. We arrived into Mumbai “waitlisted” for the evening’s train to Kochi. At the airport, a modest stand outfitted with an old computer running DOS and a dapper young man advertised “railway tickets” scratched on a cardboard sign. We waited for an hour behind a frustrated French woman who couldn’t understand how ALL of the trains were booked to EVERYWHERE. Finally, we were up. A large group of Indian men had gathered around us (Indians seem to have neither the same sense of personal space nor the same conceptions of standing in lines as do westerners). They all put in their two cents regarding how we ought to proceed. Wait there an hour and see what happens was the verdict. So, we sat and watched an unnaturally large rat scurry around the arrivals area, unnoticed by passers-by. While we finally got word that we had been moved from “waitlisted” to “confirmed”, we simultaneously found out that we might not have time to actually get to the railway station before the train departed. “How is that possible? We have over 4 hours! The station is only 40 kilometers away!”

Mumbai traffic is ugly. An unrelenting tangled mass, spewing smog, vibrating from the cacophony of hundreds of horns, no distinguishable lanes of traffic, drivers with their feet up on the steering wheel reading the paper and spitting out large wads of red betel nut, pedestrians weaving between semi-trucks and auto-rickshaws, passing them both. It makes L.A. freeways at rush hour look like the open road.

By some sheer stroke of luck, we made it to the station just in time. Our 2nd class AC berth held 6 beds. A retired musicologist and his wife who just came from visiting their daughter, son-in-law, and two grandchildren. A 24 year-old who met his fiancé for the first time in Mumbai (they met on a matrimony dating site for his particular caste – the warrior class, they chatted for a week, and then he had his parents arrange a marriage with her family. they’ve been talking for a month on the phone. he just returned from a 3 day trip to Mumbai where he first met her. he was elated. long into the evening he plied adam with questions about relationships, dating, monogamy, etc. interrupted only by frequent phone calls from his new fiancé (to whom he would whisper ‘i love you, i love you, i love you’ in english). And a philosophically oriented electrical equipment purveyor on a business trip who after 12 hours of total silence, began expounding on Hinduism: “we and god are the same in quality, we only differ in quantity. our souls are infinitesimally small– 1/100,000 of the tip of a hair. we have in fractions what krishna has in abundance. we are a drop of water, he is the ocean”.

This was India’s vibrant (and growing) middle class. Through the 24 hour train ride, we shared food, books, and stories. drinking chai, eating biriyani, and discussing the cultural particularities and universalities of marriage, divorce, relationships, feminism, communism, education, and religion.

Immersed in a world where arranged marriages are still very much the status quo, and “dating” is something from the movies rather than reality, they all struggled to understand the relationship between me and Adam. The musicologist fell back on our modern day cultural equalizer: Facebook. “Aha! You are ‘In a relationship!”‘

When the train stopped, the home-like comfort we’d created dissipated and we were back in the middle of an India whose speed was too fast and whose volume was too loud. We spent hours walking from hotel to hotel (many of which in retrospect we assume were not hotels at all) looking for a room. All full. Everywhere. Perhaps Calicut was full of Indian travelers, but without a single non-Indian face to be seen this was the moment in traveling when one begins to ask ‘are we supposed to bribe someone’?

We eventually found an over-priced, musty room in a concrete building crumbling faster than it could be repaired. At dinner people stared at us and the proprietor of the restaurant stood beside our table and watched us eat every bite. Fortunately, every bite was delicious. It seems that westerners are still novel here in Calicut.

In the morning we took a $0.40, 4 hour local bus to Sultan Bathery which abuts a wildlife sanctuary. The ride was beautiful (and treacherous) as we rounded hairpin bend after hairpin bend, ascending mountains until we were so far into the clouds that there was only white beyond, behind, in front and below us. Hundreds of monkeys sat along the road side (perhaps hopeful that someone would throw out a half eaten treat?). We entered the Wayanad Wildlife Sanctuary from the southern entrance: Muthanga. Although the experience was not comparable to our previous animal spotting experiences in Malaysia, we did see spotted deer, wild elephants, a mongoose, a sloth bear, tiger foot prints, and (as our guide called them) “common” monkeys (which we later found out to be Rhesus macaques).

Although we spent much of our time in southeast asia trying to veer from the “beaten path”, in India we’ve hardly found a path at all. Fortunately, surrounded by kind people, delicious food, and enough monkeys to keep us entertained, bushwhacking shouldn’t be a disappointment.

Central Laos: motorbike blog posts

Posted on July 10, 2012 by adam
1

After 3 liesurely days in Luang Prabang we made our way south (via back-to-back bus rides) to Tha Khaek, a small town in central Laos that sits on Laos and Thailand’s natural border, the Mekong River. Tha Khaek is our last stop in Laos before crossing to Thailand and flying out of Bangkok. Aside from being a convenient border crossing point into Thailand, Tha Khaek is also a good place to start a motorcycle journey to Kong Lo cave, a 7.5 km long cave only navigable by long boat located 180 km northeast of Tha Khaek by road.

We’d originally planned on doing a 400 km clockwise loop by motorcycle over 4 days (though, as we learn more and more each day, things rarely go as planned). We overpaid for a cheap, road worn Korean bike. It had no functioning speedometer or odometer, and a fuel gauge that dropped nearly instantly from full to empty after approximately 130 km (a feature that we only discovered 130 km into our trip), but it started most of the time and looked to be in as good condition as the rest of the haggard bikes being offered – so we took it.

We spent most of the first morning humming north on rte 13. Despite being one of Laos’s major highways, we were frequently forced to slow our speed in order to navigate around the herds of cows, goats, chickens, and dogs that also used the road to get where ever they were going (or, occasionally, as a place to take a nap). After winding our way through 40 km of mountains on rte 8 in the afternoon, we turned down a straight flat road past small villages and fields of rice paddies that stretched out to the base of the tall mountains that surrounded us. The number of vehicles on the road decreased in inverse proportion to the number of cows, which we swirved around every few kilometers as they gave us apathetic glares to let us know that we were an inconvenience to them and not the other way around. The road came to a dead end at a river rushing out of the mouth of a large cave. We’d arrived at Kong Lo.

The next morning we hired a boatman and a guide to take us the 7.5 km through the cave. As we puttered into the cave the light from the entrance shrank to a speck until we were surrounded by darkness save for our four puny headlamps. The cave was enormous, at points nearly to high for the light from my headlamp to reach the top. Our boatman navigated us around the patches of water raining from the cave’s ceiling, and after 20 minutes of drifting through the darkness we pulled our boat up next to the side of the cave and our guide got out and beckoned for us to do the same. He walked a few steps and hit a button switching on a few lights, which illuminated a couple dozen impressive stalagmites (and -tites). We snapped a few photos, our guide hit another button to turn off the lights, we got back in the boat, and continued further into the cave. After 40 minutes we saw another speck of light ahead and soon the cave opened up to a landscape of towering mountains poking their tops through a thin layer of white clouds.

Kong Lo behind us and only a day and a half into our journey, our next stop was the Nam Sanam Waterfall 40 km north of Kong Lo. We stopped for fuel a kilometer from the trail to the waterfall. As we pulled out of the petrol station Ali exclaimed, “Is that a monkey? Go back!” And, indeed, it was a monkey; a white cheeked Gibbon sitting on a stump, arms stretched behind his head lounging under an umbrella 10 meters from the gas pump. As we approached we expected him to run, but instead he climbed to the top of his stump, scratched his head, and then lowered it in my direction as if to say “now you try”. Monkey see, monkey do…

Gas in our tank and monkey on our minds, we continued on to the the waterfall. We pulled our motorcycle in to the Khoun Kham village visitor center. We were greeted by a friendly woman who told us that the waterfall was a 2 km hike from the visitor center but that the water was very high, drawing a line with her hand above her waist. “It’s ok. We brought our swim suits,” we replied confidently.

We (Ali in particular) hike at a quick pace, and would normally knock out 2 km in 30 minutes. But we underestimated the Laotian jungle in the rainy season. Two minutes from the trail’s start, as the visitor center attendant had warned, we were up to our waists in water crossing what is probably a small ditch in the dry season. “No problem” we said. “We’re going to swim under the waterfall anyway.” Our naivety began to dawn on us when after wrong turns and trails lost under recently created creeks, it began to rain as we arrived at a sign indicating that we were only half way there. The finally blow to our optimism occurred when a mere hundred meters from the waterfall the trail met a river of rushing white water. To our right was a treacherous class 6 rapid and just across the river a sign which read “You can swim” with no indication of irony. Needless to say we were unable to swim under the waterfall (we couldn’t even get close enough to see the top). We might have been disappointed, but the beautiful hike through the jungle was a reward in itself. As we drove away we could see the entire mountain we’d just hiked up and caught a glimpse of the magnificent twin waterfalls that we’d only seen in part up close.

We arrived in Lak Sao (the biggest town on our journey after Tha Khaek) just after dark, ate dinner, and found the town’s only ATM. Unfortunately the Lao Development Bank does not recognize all foreign debit cards, ours included. This may have been a matter that could have been sorted out in the bank, but we’d arrive in Lak Sao in the middle of the weekend and it wouldn’t be until monday that the bank opened. We took stock of our assets and learned that we had just enough kip to pay for the hotel and just enough dollars and Thai baht to get us home the next day. Fortunately even in the tiny town of Lak Sao there a people willing to give a bad exchange rate for dollars and baht seven days a week, so the next morning we paid for our hotel, exchanged money, and had a meager breakfast of fried dough balls and bananas before heading back to Tha Khaek the way we came. Though disappointed to be cutting our trip short, the scenery on the ride up was absolutely gorgeous (particularly the mountains along rte 8), and we were happy to get a second viewing.

On a side note, Lak Sao was the half-way point of our intended loop. We considered continuing clockwise (rather than back tracking) but had heard that the roads south of Lak Sao could be a problem if there had been a lot or rain. Our trek to the waterfall made it clear that that there had been, and, as we were already cutting it close with gas money, we decided to play it safe and stick with familiar roads. After returning to Tha Khaek we spoke to a mud soaked couple returning from a motorcycle trip similar to the one we had originally planned who told us that we’d made the right choice.

Just looking at the houses gave one a sense of vertigo. Old women walked up the steep mountain roads, carrying bags of wood, which rather than wearing across their shoulders, they strapped to their foreheads. Families sat on their porches and chatted, drinking tea and taking in the cool mountain air.

The ride made even the most motion-tolerant (read: me) queasy. Weaving around mountain sides, I was glad that our driver had neglected our need to eat. Indeed, according to road signs, in 5 1/2 hours, we went only 72 kilometers.

As we descended from the mountains, the thatched roofs of hill villages were replaced with corrugated tin, and satellite dishes dotted each neighborhood. The fog lifted and we entered flat (er) land. While I was somewhat saddened to leave behind these beautiful mountains, I couldn’t have been happier to move this 30 hour bus ride (which lingered in the present tense far too long) into the past.

At around 10 pm we arrived into Luang Prabang. The sleepy town was all dark, with the exception of Phu Si hill, which juts up from the center of the city, with the golden stupa which sits at the hill’s pinnacle illuminated.

Luang Prabang is situated at the confluence of the Khan and the Mekong rivers. Due to its unique architecture, history, and impressive concentration of Wats (or, temples), the city is a UNESCO world heritage site.

Along with an Argentinean and an American (living in Indonesia) who we met on the bus ride, we searched for somewhere to lay our weary heads. We arrived at the SySomphone guest house late. Papa and Mama (as they prefer to be called), and their two young grandchildren welcomed us with open arms. (Indeed, unlike in Vietnam, we found this gentle generosity to be the rule here rather than the exception). Papa and Mama headed to the kitchen to prepare us sandwiches and fresh banana and mango shakes. Meanwhile, 3 year-old Mieu Mieu plied us with as many bananas as could fit in our stomaches, peeling the next before we had finished the last and giggling with the delight of a true hostess.

In the morning we rented bicycles and set off to explore. We watched as fishermen navigated the Mekong in small canoes, as monks formed a procession to collect morning alms, and as women barbecued freshly caught fish on bamboo skewers. Gold and magenta temples, most of which were emblazoned with colorful statues of multi-headed dragons (some of which sprang forth from the heads of other dragons) densely dotted the town.

We sight-saw, got incredible $5 massages, visited markets. And we ate. And, boy, did we eat. Although a week ago, we couldn’t have told you what Laotian food was, we quickly found out that Laotian food -served with wicker baskets full of sticky rice -is unbelievably delicious. We ate steamed fish with lemongrass and chili, Laap (a salad of minced meat or tofu with onions, fish sauce, mint, roasted ground rice, and chilies), and green, yellow, and red curries with chicken and tofu. Despite our epicurean home-runs, we also had some culinary misses, such as a papaya salad dressed with pieces of fermented crab and its juice.

We visited Tat Kuang Si, 32 km from Luang Prabang, a breathtaking multi-layered waterfall nestled in the jungle. The limestone rock turned the water from blue to bright turquoise. We hiked up the side of the waterfall until we reached the top. Water pooled and tumbled 100 meters down, providing a stunning view of the forest and country side. The place was so wonderful, I was inclined to forgive even the dozens of leeches who came to call me dinner. We spent the rest of the day swimming in the turquoise pools, and swinging from ropes suspended above the deepest of them.

We spent another leisurely couple of days in Luang Prabang, enjoying the rain, the food, the beauty, and the people. But, as is the rule with even the most wonderful of places when traveling, itineraries trump and the road always beckons. So, off we were.

According to Vietnamese legend, a giant dragon once lived in the mountains on the northern coast of Vietnam. As it flew down the mountain to dive into the sea, its tail bounced along the tops of the hills carving out steep canyons leading into the ocean. As the pockets left by the dragon’s tail filled with shimmering emerald green water from the Gulf of Tonkin, it created a bay so magical that it gives even the most ardent skeptic pause to contemplate his disbelief in myths upon seeing its beauty. This is Halong Bay (literally, where the dragon descends into the sea).

We did little planning before leaving the States, but everything we saw and read convinced us to put Halong Bay on our list of places we had to see in Southeast Asia. However, after a negative experience on Khao San road in Bangkok (hoards of irreverent westerners whose overwhelming presence destroyed nearly all traces of authentic Thai culture) we’d decided we needed to make an effort to stray from the beaten paths suggested by most travel guide books. We’d heard that boat tours out of Halong City, while beautiful, are tarnished by both trash in the water and carts of Asian kitsch being sold to the bus loads of western tourists.

We decided instead to launch our exploration of Halong Bay and its equally beautiful neighbor, Lan Ha Bay, from the less easily accessible town of Cat Ba, located on an island in the southern part of Halong Bay. Once in Cat Ba we booked an overnight boat tour of the Bay leaving the following morning.

At 8 am we took a short bus ride to the port of departure and boarded our boat, a motorized junk with sleeping quarters, a bathroom, and a kitchen on the bottom level and an area for lounging, dining, and playing cards on the top level. As we left the crowded fishing port, we began to pass small islands of limestone rock, partially covered by green vegetation, jutting more than 100 m straight out of the water. The bay is densely populated with these limeston monuments, some like tall buildings, others like fortress walls.

In the evening after our first day on the water, the air had cooled and the sky was clear. We pulled down the sheet which had provided shade in the heat of the day and dragged a couple of mattresses from the sleeping quarters to the top deck, laid on our backs and fell asleep looking for shooting stars.

Insufficient travel research sometimes takes you to unexpected places. And with some luck, that’s not a bad thing. Our original plan was to take a bus from Inle Lake to Tachileik, a town in Burma which Lonely Planet and wikitravel.org both list as a Thailand/Burma “border crossing”. Going from Burma to Thailand requires a permit from the Burmese government which must be applied for at least two weeks in advance (i.e. well before we even knew we were going to Burma). And even if we wanted to try our luck with an illicit crossing, it’s impossible to even get to Tachileik from any other part of Burma by road. No busses, taxis, or rickshaws can take you there. Our only option: a 14 hour bus ride in the opposite direction of Laos to take a flight out of Yangon’s tiny international airport.

Disheartened by the prospect of flying further away from Laos back to Bangkok and losing 2-3 days on more cross country bus rides, we investigated alternative options. Yangon’s airport is small, but it does offer flights to a few places other than Bangkok. Maybe Beijing? Could be interesting, but we don’t have a visa. Kolkata? Probably later, but we’re not done with southeast Asia. Kuala Lumpur? Hmm…

And so, we purchased two tickets to Malaysia, knowing only that 1) it wasn’t Bangkok, and 2) tickets to there from Burma were pretty cheap. A quick skim of Frommers informed us that Borneo (an island shared by Malaysia, Indonesia, and Brunei) is the place to go for excellent scuba diving and pristine jungles. We arrived at Kuala Lumpur late Monday night, were on a plane to Borneo’s largest airport in Kota Kinabalu early Tuesday morning, and spent the rest of the day scrambling to make arrangements for the next 7 days. Borneo, slightly larger than the state of Texas, can be overwhelming without adequate planning. Add to that the fact that Sabah (Malaysia’s northern state in Borneo) is a tourist Mecca for China and Indonesia, we were lucky to squeeze in arrangements for a 4 day scuba diving course followed immediately by 3 days of boat rides and trekking through the jungles of Kinabatangan minutes before Kota Kinabalu’s booking offices closed.

At 7 the next morning we took a 2 hour van ride, followed by a 45 minute boat ride to Mantanani Island, where we would spend the next 4 days scuba diving and snorkeling. While Borneo is an island like Greenland is an island, Mantanani is more akin to Gilligan’s Island. Populated by two small villages of fishermen, the backpackers lodge we stayed at consisted of a dozen simple treehouse style bungalows just large enough for a few twin size mattresses and was the only short term sleeping accomodation on the island.

I’ll be the first to admit that vacationing at the beach is highly overrated. It’s hot, sand gets in everything, salt water feels like acid in your eyes, and the up and down and up and down of the waves is nauseating. There is so much to hate about the beach. And yet a snorkel and a pair of goggles can make it all worthwhile. Exploring a coral reef is like visiting another planet. Brilliant colors and marine life ranging from beautiful to bizarre.

Borneo is world renowned for its scuba diving sites, but most master divers skip over Mantanani, heading straight for Borneo’s more flashy dive sites. However, this neglect by the more experienced divers seems to make Mantanani an ideal spot for a beginner to master the basics of scuba diving, while enjoying the crystal clear waters of the South China Sea and all the marine life one can take in in 45 minute increments.

Becoming scuba certified (which allows for unassisted, independent scuba diving around the world) in the states can be prohibitively expensive due the cost of instructors, gear rental, and trips to divable bodies of water. Also, they can be a bit boring as much of the preliminary training on buoyancy control, breathing exercises, operating your gear, and avoiding the bends takes place in a classroom or confined water (which in the states, usually means a swimming pool). However, when scuba certification courses are taken in a place like, let’s say, Borneo, the bookwork takes place on beachside table, and the “confined water” dives 30 meters off the coast of a remote island teeming with marine life. During our 4 open water dives (far from the coast and 18 meters down) we saw sting rays, eels, crabs, scorpion fish, lion fish, star fish, clown fish, puffer fish, and many (many) other fish whose names we never learned. Floating weightlessly just above expanses of coral reefs was surreal. And the experience of breathing underwater was … well… breathtaking.

Turns out, unlike most countries where men wear western clothing while women are the carriers of culture, in Myanmar it is the men who wear traditional clothing while women dress in imported Chinese blouses and skirts. The women, however, don’t pass as entirely western. Women and children paint large circles, intricate lines, or entire masks of tha nap khar, a yellowish paste made from wood, on their faces.

We soon discovered that Myanmar does not accept cards. No one. No where. None whatsoever. One must exchange precious US dollars. And not just any dollars. Myanmar does not accept bills more than six years old, torn, worn, folded, or otherwise show any sign of use. Myanmar apparently also has a delusional conception of their currency’s worth. At the airport, government registered money changers offer an exchange rate of $1 USD for 8 Kyat, while just outside the airport doors the “black-market” offers 840 Kyat for $1 USD. So, we scrounged together whatever we had, using heavy books and moist towels in a true mafioso fashion to turn our crumpled, old dollars into something acceptable to the street-corner, back-door money changers. Alas. We largely failed.

In our two days in Yangon, we could count all the westerners we saw on our fingers. Due to the bad international press, heavy travel restrictions, and difficulty obtaining a visa, only the most intrepid travelers, or those anxious to escape the heavily trodden backpacker circuit of southeast asia, make their way to Myanmar. Billboards dot the country which read “Myanmar welcomes and takes care of tourists”. This is a message that the people have truly taken to heart. There is little to no haggling on price. We never felt taken advantage of, preyed upon, extorted or otherwise abused here. While I risk making absurd generalizations that no population could live up to, our impression was that Burmese people are open, honest, generous with their time, and curious about us. Every person we passed greeted us with a wave and a large smile, little kids ran up to dole out high fives, and mothers encouraged their toddlers to blow kisses at us. Really a refreshing change of pace for the calloused traveler, always on guard, always cynical.

We could also count all of the overt signs of a military presence: Zero. For a country known internationally for a military that abuses, rapes, murders, silences, and systematically strips away rights from its people, the strong arm of the state was illusive. Most shops openly posted photographs of Aung San Suu Kyi, who, likened to Nelson Mandela and having received the Nobel Peace Prize, is a national hero and represents hope for political reform. We even witnessed a protest. At Shwedagon Paya, a large (and astoundingly beautiful) temple complex in the center of the city, Buddhist monks (who have historically been loud and visible in their dissent against the government) and lay people from Rakhin state condemned the government for not protecting its people from politically and religiously motivated acts of violence (allegedly) committed by Bangladeshis on the two nations’ border.

Along with being on the front line for political reform, monks, clad in flowing red robes, continued to shatter our preconceptions of serene, docile, and otherworldly beings. In Myanmar,which is described as the most devout Buddhist country in the world, all boys and men are expected to shave their heads, don robes, and pick up alms bowls at least twice in their lives, once in boyhood, and once after becoming a man. As a result, the line between monks and lay people is fuzzy. Many monks smoke cigarettes, are covered in tattoos, and spend their days watching soccer and eating snacks with their buddies. It took us a long time to become accustomed to the sight of a monk reclining at a street-side cafe, eating peanuts and giggling with friends. Yes, perhaps I could be a monk when I grow up…

bangkok: the cancun of southeast asia

Posted on June 8, 2012 by ali
2

After our 4th overnight flight in 6 days, we arrived in Bangkok to meet up with Adam’s friend, Kasey. From the airport we sped past imposing skyscrapers and sprawling apartment complexes (occasionally dotted with an imposing Buddha statue or temple). There was no filth, chaos, or disarray to be seen. Manicured and high tech, Bangkok presents itself to the world.

And so we arrived in the backpacker’s ghetto, Khao San. Every business in this area is catered to tourists: massage parlors, restaurants, bars, clubs, hotels, internet cafes, shopping centers, coffee shops. Everything is open. You are invited (nay, commanded) in everywhere. Bright multi-colored lights twinkle, loud reggae or lady gaga blares from every speaker, every ten feet a big neon sign announces drink specials, tours, or sex shows. loose fitting tank tops and tight t-shirts with drawings of bicycles and vintage cameras, screen prints of kubrick’s “full metal jacket”, or of a naked woman snorting coke which forms the letters of “facebook”, even banksey prints, spill over into the road. Ironic and aloof – straight from an urban outfitter’s catalogue. meanwhile, hordes of young, attractive, scantily clad westerners roam aimlessly drinking and eating, buying, smoking, nursing hang-overs, and hitting on one another.

From this angle, it appears that bangkok not only thrives on tourism – tourists are bangkok . Indeed, Thais do not appear to be consuming Bangkok at all – their only role here is of purveyors of food, booze, massages, whatever you need… Strange to visit a place where the people remain invisible.

Tourists gather together in bars and cafes, swapping stories of how they’ve been scammed, rolling their eyes, sighing — “ugh. thailand!”. An open hostility between tourists and thais is palpable. A sign in our hostel says “We do not allow Thai people to go upstairs. No matter the reason”. Not only are the tourists convinced that Thais are scammers, hustlers, and thieves, apparently so is the Thai management (or at least they are complicit in perpetuating this image). There is something repulsive about the tourist who comes to visit a country only to fear and desire containment from it’s population.

One problem with a place like this is that when we are surrounded by people who are just like us, we forget that maybe the rules ought to change a bit, we forget that we are playing on someone else’s field. And so, the question remains, what is the point of traveling? Why do we spend thousands of dollars to fly across oceans when we only want to replicate home? an inherent ambivalence of the human spirit, the desire to see, feel, touch and taste what lies beyond, and yet the (understandable) desire to stay where we are comfortable?

But, walk a mile from this place, and the white faces, short shorts, and enormous backpacks begin to disappear, replaced by a bustling city, grocery stores, and old women who bow to greet one another . Alas! There is a real city beyond the neon glare!

Yesterday we explored the residential streets, local markets, and public spaces of Thai bangkok. We stumbled across hundreds of tents in front of the parliament building. In celebration of the day of Vesak, Buddha’s 2,600th year from attaining enlightenment, a group had gathered to cook and share free food for everyone. Rather than being met as outsiders, the people invited us to join, took us from stall to stall, encouraging us to share a meal. Old women tugged me from one place to another, filling my arms with rambutan (a red fruit native to here covered in spikes and filled with a lychee-like jelly), popcorn, noodles, and bananas. There was a large stage where people spoke, danced, and sung. Big bags of rice and other food staples were being handed out for free to those in need.

And so, with our faiths somewhat restored, we headed back to the neon, and yes, ended the night with a $3 foot massage.

A sleepless night of packing, followed by two consecutive nights of dozing in airports and planes, we arrived in Mumbai exhausted in the early evening of our third full day of traveling. We splurged and spent an extra $2 on a taxi with AC. Worth every rupee. The hour long ride to our hotel was both dreadful and amazing. With automobiles so tightly packed, I could have, at any time, reached over and honked the horn of the car next to us. Please keep your arms in the vehicle at all times. If you want to keep them that is. After depositing our packs at the Royal Castle Hotel, we went out to take in the city. Mumbai is fantastic. Vibrant, dirty, teaming with people, and nearly apathetic of our existence.

Today we awoke early (unable to sleep due to jet lag) and did a bit more roaming, looking for something delicious for breakfast and a strong cup of coffee. Afterwards we packed up our belongings and grabbed a taxi to the India Guest House in Colaba. The 20 minute, un-air conditioned ride made me glad we’d opted for AC on the previous day. After checking in, we again hit the city, wandering past the famous Taj Mahal Hotel and into the Gateway of India where we arranged for a taxi to drive us to all of the obligatory tourist stops: Dhobi Ghaat, open air laundry; Worli Fishing Village; Mani Bhavan Gandhi Museum; etc… We came back to the hotel early and took a long, much need nap, before heading out for our first true Indian dining experience. Paneer Tikka Masala for me and Vegetable Korma for Ali. Absolutely delicious.
